Thesis Update Three platform shifts — why Nvidia's position is structural, not cyclical

Most investors look at Nvidia through a single lens — AI chips, hyperscaler spending, GPU demand. What makes the current moment genuinely different is that three foundational transitions in computing are unfolding simultaneously, and Nvidia is positioned at the centre of all three.

The first is the shift from general-purpose CPUs to accelerated computing. This is not a product cycle — it is a permanent architectural change in how data centres are built. Every dollar of CPU-era infrastructure that gets replaced with accelerated compute is a Nvidia revenue opportunity, and that rebuild is measured in decades, not quarters.

The second is the shift from retrieval-based software to generative AI. The economics here are different from any prior software cycle: inference demand scales with usage in a way that retrieval never did. Every token generated is compute consumed. As AI applications proliferate, the baseline demand for Nvidia's hardware grows with them.

The third is agentic AI and physical intelligence — autonomous systems, robotics, simulation. This is the earliest stage of the three shifts, but it represents a compute demand opportunity that is potentially larger than the first two combined. Nvidia's investments in simulation platforms and physical AI infrastructure suggest they are building for this before the market prices it in.

These are not sequential trends — they are compounding simultaneously. That convergence is what drove 65% EPS growth and is what underpins the case for 70%+ next.
Investors who frame Nvidia as an AI hype trade are applying a cyclical framework to what is fundamentally a multi-decade infrastructure rebuild.

Most AI bubble fears are really training fears. The concern is that hyperscalers are overbuilding training infrastructure ahead of uncertain monetisation, creating a capex cycle that cannot be justified by actual AI revenue. Nebius's Token Factory — treating inference as a manufacturing problem with predictable per-token pricing, latency guarantees, and reliability SLAs — is how they compete on this ground. It is a fundamentally different product from renting raw GPU hours. The software orchestration layer, the managed ClickHouse integration for zero-latency data access, the AI-engineer support model — these are all inference-oriented differentiators that create product stickiness hyperscalers cannot easily replicate at the specialist level.

Hyperscalers will not automatically dominate inference. They optimise for breadth and general-purpose access. Enterprise inference at scale requires specialist engineering depth, latency optimisation, and the kind of hands-on AI engineering support that hyperscalers are structurally unable to provide at the customer level.
The coexistence thesis: hyperscalers and specialists like Nebius serve different customer profiles. Enterprises with complex, production-grade AI inference requirements often prefer specialists. That market does not disappear just because AWS and Google Cloud exist.
